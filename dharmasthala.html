Brief history of Temple

The history of Dharmasthala, a renowned Hindu pilgrimage site in Karnataka, India, reflects a blend of spirituality and local tradition:

Mythological origins
Dharmasthala is known for the temple dedicated to Lord Manjunatha (a form of Lord Shiva), and it holds a unique place in Hindu culture for its association with both Shaiva and Vaishnava traditions. According to legend, the site was once called Kuduma, and the temple's origin is tied to the Jain Chieftain Birmanna Pergade. The guardian angels of Dharma are believed to have appeared in Pergade's dreams, asking him to build a shrine dedicated to them. Later, Lord Manjunatha appeared to Pergade in a dream, directing him to install a Shiva Linga. The temple's roots in charity, justice, and religious tolerance have made Dharmasthala a spiritual haven for people of all faiths.

Construction
The main Manjunatha Temple was built centuries ago and has been renovated over time. The architecture of the temple reflects Dravidian style, with a simple yet majestic stone structure. The temple also houses a prominent idol of Lord Manjunatha, along with shrines for Lord Ganapati, the Dharma Daivas, and other deities. In addition to its religious significance, Dharmasthala is known for its philanthropic activities and continues to serve as a center of religious and social welfare.

Location
Dharmasthala is located in the Belthangady Taluk of Dakshina Kannada district in Karnataka. Nestled in the Western Ghats, the temple complex is surrounded by scenic beauty, offering a peaceful and spiritual environment for pilgrims. The region is also known for its rich cultural heritage and charitable institutions that serve people across caste, creed, and religion.



Best time to get here

The best time to visit Dharmasthala, a prominent pilgrimage site in Karnataka, India, is between October and March:

Weather
During these months, the weather is pleasant with temperatures ranging between 20–32°C. The post-monsoon period ensures a lush green landscape and clear skies, making it an ideal time for pilgrimage and sightseeing.

Activities
Visitors can participate in the daily rituals at the Manjunatha Temple, witness the cultural and religious activities, and explore the nearby attractions such as the Bahubali statue at Ratnagiri Hill and the Netravati River. Dharmasthala is also known for its Annadanam (free food offering) that feeds thousands of devotees daily as part of its charitable services.
